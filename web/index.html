Q&A

What are Zika symptoms?

Symptoms can include fever, rash, joint pain, and red eyes. Most people infected with Zika do not develop symptoms, and any symptoms may occur several days after the infection. Those infected with Zika usually don’t have to be hospitalized, and they very rarely die from Zika. However, a Zika infection during pregnancy can cause microcephaly, a serious birth defect where a baby’s brain and head do not properly develop and the baby is born with a small head.

How to prevent Zika?

No vaccines currently exist to prevent Zika. You can reduce your risks by protecting yourself from mosquito bites, since virus-infected mosquitoes are the primary way that Zika gets transmitted. Using insect repellant is one great way to prevent mosquito bites. Clearing up mosquito-breeding sites like trash or standing water can also be effective.

How to treat Zika?

No special medicine currently exists for Zika. You can treat the symptoms by getting plenty of rest and drinking fluids. Zika usually remains in the blood of an infected person for about a week, but it can be found longer in some people. An infected person is likely to develop immunity from Zika and be protected from future infections.

Where are Zika cases?

In the United States, there have been 618 cases reported, all of whom have been associated with traveling to countries with a higher risk of Zika transmissions. Of those 618 travel-associated cases, 11 cases are believed to be sexually transmitted. Countries in South America, Central America, and the Pacific have higher risk of Zika transmissions.

How does Zika spread?

A person infected with the Zika can inflect someone else during the first week of infection. The virus is found in the blood and can be passed from a person to a mosquito, and from an infected mosquito to another person. The Zika virus can also be transmitted through unprotected sex, though this is less common than transmission through mosquitoes.

History of Zika: How did it appear?

In 1947, research scientists in Uganda first discovered the Zika virus on a monkey in the Zika Forest. No large outbreaks of Zika were reported until 2007, when an epidemic occurred on Yap Island in Micronesia. Zika is related to other mosquito-transmittable viruses like dengue, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis, and West Nile viruses. Zika did not begin spreading widely in the Western Hemisphere until May, when an outbreak occurred in Brazil. For most people, a Zika infection causes no symptoms and leads to no lasting harm. Scientific concern is focused on women who become infected while pregnant, since Zika can cause microcephaly, a serious birth defect where a baby’s brain and head do not properly develop and the baby is born with a small head.
